\begin{abstract}
    Establishing Ramsey number lower bounds by finding n-vertex graphs that avoid large cliques and large independent sets constitutes a well-studied class of hard combinatorial feasibility problems. Known bounds have accumulated over decades through problem-specific discrete heuristics, with several key values remaining out of reach for generic methods. Recent LLM-driven evolutionary search using AlphaEvolve (Nagda et al., 2026) follows the same pattern: it obtained new off-diagonal bounds but stayed entirely within the discrete paradigm.

    We bypass discrete search entirely. Casting the construction problem as minimization of a pseudo-Boolean polynomial violation count and relaxing the binary constraints to the unit hypercube yields a smooth multilinear objective. Frank-Wolfe performs surprisingly well on this relaxation: its linear minimization oracle over the hypercube returns binary vertices, driving reliable progress despite the objective being non-monotone supermodular. The connection to Continuous Greedy — which exploits the same linear oracle for monotone submodular maximization — offers some intuition as to why. A conditional-expectations rounding converts the fractional solution to a binary one without increasing the violation count. This pipeline recovers a significant portion of the known lower-bound table, most notably finding 42-vertex graphs certifying $R(5,5) \geq 43$, a bound standing since 1989 that AlphaEvolve did not recover.
\end{abstract}



\section{Introduction}

Hard combinatorial feasibility problems can often be reformulated as smooth optimization:
moving constraints into the objective as penalty terms and relaxing binary variables to the
unit hypercube yields a differentiable problem accessible to first-order methods. This has
been well studied for structured objective classes: Continuous Greedy provides approximation
guarantees for monotone submodular maximization by exploiting a linear oracle that returns
vertices of the feasible polytope at each step \cite{vondrak2008optimal,calinescu2011maximizing}.
We are interested in the complementary regime of non-monotone supermodular
objectives, where the classical monotone Continuous Greedy theory is no longer the governing
model, but the linear-oracle geometry remains available. We explore Frank-Wolfe~\cite{braun2022conditional}
for this setting: each step reduces to the same linear vertex-returning oracle as Continuous
Greedy, but the update rules differ in a way that matters. Continuous Greedy
accumulates additive steps from zero, a design that exploits and requires monotonicity; Frank-Wolfe
instead takes a convex combination of the current iterate and the oracle vertex, allowing
coordinates to move in either direction. In the generic nonconvex setting, Frank-Wolfe only
achieves stationarity guarantees, so the question is whether this update rule is useful for
producing certifiable discrete witnesses.
We study this on Ramsey lower-bound search as a concrete, exactly verifiable test case.

The Ramsey number $R(r,s)$ is the critical graph order at which every graph must contain either a clique of size $r$ or an independent set of size $s$; certifying $R(r,s) > n$ requires producing a graph on $n$ vertices with clique number strictly less than $r$ and independence number strictly less than $s$. Several key values have resisted decades of effort: most notably, $R(5,5)$ is known to lie between 43 and 46, with the lower bound standing since Exoo (1989) despite sustained search~\cite{exoo1989lower}. Prior constructive lower-bound approaches --- local search heuristics such as simulated annealing and tabu search, extensions of known Ramsey graphs, and constructions restricted to algebraic families such as circulant or Cayley graphs --- have largely operated in discrete search spaces. Recent LLM-driven evolutionary search with AlphaEvolve produces new off-diagonal bounds while still relying on generated discrete search programs~\cite{nagda2026reinforcedgenerationcombinatorialstructures}. Progress in this setting takes three distinct forms. The most direct is improving a known lower bound by exhibiting a witness on more vertices than any prior construction. Less direct but still informative is finding new extremal witnesses at the same order: classifications of frontier Ramsey graphs remain far from complete, and each new construction enriches the structural picture. Recovering known witnesses through a markedly different method also matters: it tests whether the method reaches the relevant part of the search space without being tailored to known constructions, and provides computational evidence about how thoroughly that space has been explored.

We apply the continuous-relaxation approach to Ramsey lower-bound search. The Ramsey constraints become a polynomial penalty on the edge variables; relaxing those variables from binary to the unit interval yields a smooth optimization problem on a convex domain. The linear oracle on the hypercube takes the simplest possible form: at each iterate, it returns the binary graph determined by the coordinatewise sign of the gradient, so each Frank-Wolfe step pulls the iterate toward a candidate discrete witness. The penalty is not in the monotone submodular regime exploited by standard Continuous Greedy: the clique and independent-set terms are both supermodular, but have opposite monotonicities, making the raw objective non-monotone. In our experiments we optimize a log-barrier surrogate of this objective, which empirically sharpens gradient signal in regions where the raw multilinear gradient is small. The continuous iterate itself admits a combinatorial reading: with each edge weight interpreted as the probability that the corresponding edge appears, the violation objective is exactly the expected number of cliques of size $r$ and independent sets of size $s$ in the resulting random graph. Conditional-expectations rounding derandomizes this expectation by fixing edges one at a time, extracting a binary candidate without increasing the violation count. This connects the continuous optimization phrasing to the probabilistic method for Ramsey lower bounds, a viewpoint introduced by Erd\H{o}s in the 1940s and now foundational in extremal combinatorics~\cite{erdos1947remarks}.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item We formulate Ramsey lower-bound search as minimization of a smooth multilinear violation objective over the hypercube and identify the non-monotone supermodular structure of this relaxation.
    \item We develop a projection-free Frank-Wolfe pipeline for this objective, run without algebraic restrictions: the hypercube linear oracle returns binary graphs at each iterate, stochastic clique batching enables scalable gradient estimation, and conditional-expectations rounding with exact verification converts relaxed iterates into certified witnesses.
    \item We show empirically that this pipeline recovers a broad set of known Ramsey lower-bound witnesses, including repeated recovery of $42$-vertex graphs certifying $R(5,5)>42$, a long-standing frontier construction not reported by the recent AlphaEvolve Ramsey search.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Related Literature.}
Frank-Wolfe methods are projection-free first-order algorithms for smooth optimization over convex sets with efficient linear minimization oracles~\cite{frank1956algorithm,jaggi2013revisiting,braun2022conditional}. Variants such as away-step and pairwise Frank-Wolfe modify the active-set dynamics and can obtain stronger convergence guarantees under additional convexity and geometric assumptions~\cite{lacostejulien2015global}. Our use is closest to the basic nonconvex conditional-gradient viewpoint: over the edge hypercube, the linear oracle is separable and returns a binary graph, while generic theory supplies stationarity or Frank-Wolfe-gap guarantees rather than approximation or feasibility guarantees.

Continuous Greedy is the canonical multilinear-extension method for submodular maximization. Vondr{\'a}k introduced the continuous-greedy framework for submodular welfare~\cite{vondrak2008optimal}, and Calinescu, Chekuri, P{\'a}l, and Vondr{\'a}k developed the multilinear-extension approach for monotone submodular maximization under matroid constraints, including rounding with a $(1-1/e)$ guarantee~\cite{calinescu2011maximizing}. Pipage rounding and contention-resolution schemes are central fractional-to-integral tools in this line of work~\cite{ageev2004pipage,chekuri2011contention}; our extraction step is different, using conditional expectations over the independent-edge model on the full hypercube. The connection is therefore methodological rather than theorem-level: our pipeline shares the multilinear relaxation, linear-oracle geometry, and fractional-to-discrete extraction motif, but the raw Ramsey objective is a non-monotone supermodular violation-minimization objective rather than a monotone submodular utility.

Non-monotone submodular maximization has its own algorithmic literature, including randomized and DoubleGreedy-style algorithms for unconstrained nonnegative objectives~\cite{feige2011maximizing,buchbinder2015tight}. The continuous-greedy-type methods are more directly adjacent after sign-flipping the Ramsey violation objective. Feldman, Naor, and Schwartz introduced a unified continuous-greedy framework for nonnegative non-monotone submodular maximization over down-monotone polytopes~\cite{feldman2011unified}. Bian et al.\ study continuous submodular and DR-submodular maximization; their monotone result has a Frank-Wolfe/Continuous-Greedy flavor, while their non-monotone box-constrained method is DoubleGreedy-style and coordinatewise rather than linear-oracle-driven~\cite{bian2017guaranteed}. Mokhtari, Hassani, and Karbasi develop stochastic conditional-gradient methods, including non-monotone stochastic continuous greedy for DR-submodular maximization~\cite{mokhtari2020stochastic}. These works identify relevant baselines once the violation objective is sign-flipped and shifted to be nonnegative, whereas we optimize the raw violation objective with vanilla Frank-Wolfe over the full edge hypercube, combining fractional iterates and binary oracle outputs with conditional-expectations rounding and exhaustive verification.

We defer the detailed Ramsey chronology, bound table, and comparison with prior constructive methods to \cref{sec:problem}.

Discrete optimization problems in extremal combinatorics often have a useful asymmetry: finding an optimum may be difficult, but checking a proposed witness can be exact and cheap relative to the search. Ramsey lower bounds are a canonical example. To prove $R(r,s)>n$, it suffices to exhibit a graph on $n$ vertices with no clique of size $r$ and no independent set of size $s$, or equivalently a two-coloring of the edges of the complete graph $K_n$ with no forbidden monochromatic clique. This makes Ramsey search a useful benchmark for optimization heuristics: the search space has size $2^{\binom{n}{2}}$, the landscape is highly nonconvex and high-order, and every successful output is independently verifiable.

Recent computational progress in extremal combinatorics has relied on a broad spectrum of ideas, including classical local search, cross-entropy methods, neural constructions, and LLM-assisted heuristic discovery \cite{Rubinstein2004,wagner2021constructions,nagda2026reinforcedgenerationcombinatorialstructures}. These methods often exploit restrictions of the search space, such as algebraic, circulant, vertex-transitive, Cayley, or vertex-by-vertex constructions, which can reduce the number of effective variables from quadratic in $n$ to nearly linear. Such restrictions are powerful, but they raise the usual question of whether the imposed structure excludes the relevant optima. The complementary approach we study here is to keep the full edge-variable search space and relax the discrete problem into a differentiable one.

Our guiding question is therefore not whether continuous optimization replaces specialized Ramsey search, but whether a standard first-order method can serve as a broadly useful discrete-optimization heuristic on this hard, exactly certified task. Frank-Wolfe methods are attractive in this role because their linear minimization oracle over a hypercube or product of simplices returns an extreme point. Each update is projection-free, inexpensive once gradients are available, and geometrically biased toward the discrete colorings that ultimately need to be certified \cite{frank1956algorithm,braun2022conditional}. This is the main narrative of the paper: Ramsey search exposes a setting where conditional gradients are not merely a way to solve a relaxation, but a mechanism for steering a continuous iterate toward useful combinatorial witnesses.

We structure the paper as follows. \cref{eq:no-monochromatic-cliques} turns Ramsey search into a pseudo-Boolean polynomial objective; the continuous relaxation gives a probabilistic interpretation as the expected number of forbidden cliques; the surrogate losses reshape the gradients around near-violations; Frank-Wolfe supplies a simple projection-free update; stochastic clique batching makes the method scalable; and conditional expectation plus exhaustive checking turns relaxed candidates into certified graphs. We refer to \cite{morris2026recentresultsramseytheory} for recent Ramsey-theoretic background.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item We frame Ramsey lower-bound search as a testbed for Frank-Wolfe type methods as heuristics for hard discrete and combinatorial optimization.
  \item We connect the classical satisfiability view of Ramsey witnesses to a pseudo-Boolean clique-counting objective and its natural probabilistic continuous relaxation.
  \item We use clique-wise surrogate losses, including a log-barrier variant, to emphasize potentially monochromatic cliques while preserving the structure of the original objective.
  \item We describe a projection-free conditional-gradient pipeline with stochastic clique batching, deterministic conditional-expectation extraction, and exact verification.
  \item Empirically, the method recovers strong diagonal and near-diagonal witnesses, including repeated recovery of $42$-vertex colorings certifying $R(5,5)>42$.
\end{itemize}


\section{Problem}\label{sec:problem}

\subsection{Ramsey Search as Discrete Optimization}

Let $K_n=(V,E)$ be the complete graph on $n$ vertices, and write $m=\binom{n}{2}$ for the number of edges. A two-coloring can be represented by a vector
\[
x=(x_{ij})_{1\leq i<j\leq n}\in \{0,1\}^{\binom{n}{2}},
\]
where $x_{ij}=1$ denotes that edge $\{i,j\}$ receives the first color and $x_{ij}=0$ the second. For integers $r,s \geq 2$, the Ramsey number $R(r,s)$ is the smallest $n$ such that every coloring of $K_n$ contains either a monochromatic $K_r$ in the first color or a monochromatic $K_s$ in the second. Equivalently, a certificate for the lower bound $R(r,s)>n$ is a graph on $n$ vertices with no $r$-clique and no independent set of size $s$.

The problem is often stated as a satisfiability question: does there exist a coloring satisfying all constraints that forbid these cliques? We use the standard optimization reformulation that replaces hard constraints by a pseudo-Boolean polynomial objective. Let $\mathcal{C}_k$ denote the family of $k$-vertex subsets of $V$. Define
\begin{equation}\label{eq:no-monochromatic-cliques}
    M(x) = \sum_{C \in \mathcal{C}_r} \prod_{\{i,j\} \subseteq C} x_{ij}
    \quad + \quad
    \sum_{C \in \mathcal{C}_s} \prod_{\{i,j\} \subseteq C} \left(1 - x_{ij}\right).
\end{equation}
The first sum counts monochromatic $K_r$ subgraphs in color $1$, and the second counts monochromatic $K_s$ subgraphs in color $0$. Thus $M(x)=0$ if and only if $x$ is a valid witness for $R(r,s)>n$. The corresponding discrete optimization problem is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:opt-problem}
    \min_{x \in \{0,1\}^{\binom{n}{2}}} M(x).
\end{equation}
This formulation already performs one relaxation of the satisfiability view: hard clauses become a smooth multilinear penalty on binary variables. From an optimization perspective, the next step is to relax integrality.






\section{Method}\label{sec:method}

We solve the relaxed problem with a Frank-Wolfe scheme. The order of presentation in this section follows the order in which the design choices accumulate. We first introduce the continuous relaxation and note the probabilistic interpretation it inherits (Section~\ref{sec:probabilistic-relaxation}). We then discuss the loss the optimizer actually operates on, because the raw multilinear objective gives a poor first-order signal and motivates a clique-wise log-barrier surrogate (Section~\ref{sec:surrogate}). With the loss fixed, we describe the Frank-Wolfe update and its closed-form linear minimization oracle (Section~\ref{sec:fw-updates}), the stochastic clique batching that makes the method scalable (Section~\ref{sec:batching}), and the deterministic conditional-expectation rounding that turns relaxed iterates into certified discrete witnesses (Section~\ref{sec:extraction}).

\subsection{Probabilistic Continuous Relaxation}\label{sec:probabilistic-relaxation}

The discrete feasible set in \cref{eq:opt-problem} admits a natural continuous relaxation:

\begin{equation}\label{eq:relaxed-problem}
    \min_{x \in [0,1]^m} M(x).
\end{equation}

Under this relaxation, each variable $x_{ij}$ can be interpreted as the probability that edge $(i,j)$ receives color $1$ under an independent Bernoulli coloring model. The objective $M(x)$ is then the expected number of forbidden monochromatic cliques, since each product term is exactly the probability that a given clique is monochromatic in one of the two colors. This probabilistic view preserves a direct connection to the discrete search problem while exposing a continuous domain on which first-order methods can operate.

Equivalently, the relaxed problem can be written as

\begin{align}\label{eq:constrained-problem}
    \min_{x} \quad &\sum_{C \in \mathcal{C}_r} \prod_{\{i,j\} \subseteq C} x_{ij}
    \quad + \quad
    \sum_{C \in \mathcal{C}_s} \prod_{\{i,j\} \subseteq C} \left(1 - x_{ij}\right), \\
    \text{s.t.} \quad &x \in [0,1]^m.
\end{align}

The objective is a multilinear polynomial of degree $\binom{\max(r,s)}{2}$ in $m=\binom{n}{2}$ variables, so gradients can be computed explicitly whenever clique enumeration is feasible. Although any final certificate must still be discrete, the continuous relaxation is useful because it exposes informative descent directions and allows the optimizer to exploit partial progress before a valid witness has fully polarized.

This relaxation is natural from two perspectives. In extremal combinatorics, it is the probabilistic variant of the coloring problem. In discrete optimization, it is the passage from SAT-style hard constraints to a pseudo-Boolean objective and then to an integrality relaxation. The important caveat is that fractional progress is useful only insofar as it can be extracted into a binary coloring and verified directly.


\subsection{Clique-Wise Surrogate Losses}\label{sec:surrogate}

The guarantees above apply to the raw expected-count objective $M$. This objective is conceptually natural, but in practice it provides a weak optimization signal. Because each clique contribution is a high-order product of numbers in $[0,1]$, the raw objective often yields very small gradients even when a candidate solution is close to creating a forbidden monochromatic clique. As a result, a first-order method can spend many iterations reducing already-safe regions of the graph while paying insufficient attention to the most dangerous near-violations.

To make this issue explicit, consider the raw expected-count objective
\begin{equation}\label{eq:linear_expectation}
    M(x)
    =
    \sum_{C \in \mathcal{C}_r} \prod_{\{i,j\} \subseteq C} x_{ij}
    +
    \sum_{C \in \mathcal{C}_s} \prod_{\{i,j\} \subseteq C} (1 - x_{ij}).
\end{equation}
When a clique differs from a monochromatic one by only a small number of edges, the corresponding product term can still be numerically tiny, especially for larger clique sizes. The optimizer is then encouraged to distribute mass diffusely rather than to eliminate the specific substructures most likely to invalidate a witness.

We therefore optimize a barrier-style surrogate that penalizes each potentially monochromatic clique individually:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:log_barrier}
    L(x)
    =
    -\sum_{C \in \mathcal{C}_r}
    \log\left(1 - \prod_{\{i,j\} \subseteq C} x_{ij}\right)
    -
    \sum_{C \in \mathcal{C}_s}
    \log\left(1 - \prod_{\{i,j\} \subseteq C} (1 - x_{ij})\right).
\end{equation}
The logarithmic transformation changes the geometry of the problem in a useful way. Each clique behaves like an individual soft constraint, and the penalty for a nearly monochromatic clique grows rapidly as its monochromatic probability approaches $1$. We emphasize that, despite the superficial resemblance, this is genuinely different from a classical interior-point log-barrier: a barrier method repels iterates from the boundary of the feasible set, whereas here it is the forbidden monochromatic subgraphs that are repelled. The values $0$ and $1$ on individual edges are not penalized at all; the optimizer is allowed, and in fact encouraged, to move toward discrete edge colorings, as long as no clique becomes fully monochromatic in the process.

\paragraph{Gradient clipping.}
The logarithmic surrogate has an important implementation caveat: it is undefined when any clique is monochromatic with probability $1$. This can occur not only from numerical saturation, but also by design when the Frank-Wolfe search is combined with a discrete heuristic that snaps fractional edge probabilities to a binary coloring. In those cases we replace each monochromatic clique probability $p$ by $\min(p,1-\varepsilon)$ before evaluating $-\log(1-p)$, for a small $\varepsilon>0$. The forward objective is then finite and the largest per-clique penalty is controlled by $-\log \varepsilon$.

There remains a choice about the gradient through this clipping operation. With the ordinary clamp, cliques whose probability exceeds $1-\varepsilon$ have zero derivative with respect to $p$; this is numerically stable, but it also removes the optimization signal from precisely the saturated constraints that caused the problem. We therefore also consider a straight-through variant: the forward pass uses the clipped value, while the backward pass treats the clipping operation as the identity. This preserves the descent direction of the original product term and caps the scalar log factor at $1/\varepsilon$, at the cost of using a biased gradient estimator rather than the exact derivative of the clipped objective. We treat this as an optimization heuristic, and report the clipping threshold and gradient convention together with the loss function in experiments.

As the highlighted triangle becomes nearly monochromatic, the raw product term $p_{\mathrm{mono}}$ remains bounded and changes smoothly, while $-\log(1-p_{\mathrm{mono}})$ grows sharply, concentrating optimization pressure on the dangerous near-violation.}

We treat the choice of surrogate as a design decision rather than a theorem-level necessity. Recent experiments suggest that, with suitable hyperparameters, the vanilla probability loss can also recover certificates for $R(5,5)$ on $42$ vertices. The log-barrier objective remains useful as a principled way to emphasize near-violations, but the results should ultimately include an ablation separating the effect of the surrogate from the effect of the optimizer and batching strategy.

\subsection{Frank-Wolfe Updates}\label{sec:fw-updates}

We optimize the relaxed objective over the hypercube $[0,1]^m$ with a Frank-Wolfe type method \cite{frank1956algorithm,braun2022conditional}. At iteration $t$, given a current iterate $x_t$, the linear minimization subproblem is
\begin{equation}
    s_t \in \arg\min_{s \in [0,1]^m} \langle \nabla L(x_t), s \rangle.
\end{equation}
Because the feasible region is a hypercube, this oracle has a simple closed form:
\begin{equation}
    (s_t)_{ij} =
    \begin{cases}
        0, & \text{if } \frac{\partial L(x_t)}{\partial x_{ij}} > 0, \\
        1, & \text{otherwise.}
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}

The iterate is then updated by
\begin{equation}
    x_{t+1} = (1-\gamma_t)x_t + \gamma_t s_t,
\end{equation}
where $\gamma_t \in [0,1]$ is a step size.

This choice is attractive for two reasons. First, each update is projection-free and inexpensive once the gradient has been estimated. Second, the linear minimization oracle always returns a vertex of the hypercube, so the iterates are repeatedly pulled toward discrete colorings rather than remaining in the interior. In our setting this bias toward extreme points is desirable because the final goal is a binary witness, not merely a low-value continuous relaxation.

All experiments reported below use this vanilla Frank-Wolfe update with a standard diminishing step-size schedule. We also implemented a pairwise Frank-Wolfe variant, which moves probability mass directly from an ``away'' atom $a_t \in \arg\max_{a \in \mathcal{A}(x_t)} \langle \nabla L(x_t), a \rangle$ to the Frank-Wolfe atom $s_t$ via $x_{t+1} = x_t + \gamma_t (s_t - a_t)$, with the step size capped by the current weight of $a_t$ to keep the iterate feasible. On small instances the pairwise variant converged noticeably faster than vanilla Frank-Wolfe, but on $R(5,5)$ at $n=42$ we did not obtain reliable convergence with it at the budgets we tested; we therefore report vanilla Frank-Wolfe as our main method and leave a careful study of pairwise and away-step variants at this scale to future work.

\paragraph{Remark (multicolor parameterization).}
Although the presentation above is two-color, the implementation actually operates on a multicolor parameterization in which each edge carries a probability vector $x_{ij} \in \Delta^{c-1}$ over colors. The linear minimization oracle is unchanged in spirit: writing $g_{ij} \in \mathbb{R}^c$ for the gradient on edge $\{i,j\}$, $\arg\min_{z \in \Delta^{c-1}} \langle g_{ij}, z \rangle$ places all mass on a minimizing color, i.e.\ returns a standard basis vector. The oracle still returns an extreme point, now interpreted as choosing one color per edge. This keeps the code compatible with multicolor Ramsey variants, but plays no role in the experiments reported here, all of which are two-color.

\subsection{Stochastic Clique Batching}\label{sec:batching}

The main computational bottleneck is clique enumeration. Even for moderate $n$, the number of $k$-cliques,
\[
|\mathcal{C}_k| = \binom{n}{k},
\]
is far too large for exact evaluation at every Frank-Wolfe step. Since our objectives are sums of independent clique-wise penalties, the natural approximation is to replace the full sum by a mini-batch average.

Let $\ell_r(C;x)$ and $\ell_s(C;x)$ denote the contribution of clique $C$ to the two color classes. For the barrier surrogate in \cref{eq:log_barrier},
\[
\ell_r(C;x) = -\log\left(1 - \prod_{\{i,j\} \subseteq C} x_{ij}\right),
\qquad
\ell_s(C;x) = -\log\left(1 - \prod_{\{i,j\} \subseteq C} (1-x_{ij})\right).
\]
In diagonal instances, both terms are indexed by the same family of cliques, so each stochastic step samples a batch $S\subseteq \mathcal{C}_r$ and evaluates both colors on the same local vertex patterns. In off-diagonal instances, the two terms live on different clique families; our implementation synchronizes independent batches across epochs so that both color classes complete one full pass through their respective clique families at the same time. The detailed batching construction is given in the appendix.
\subsection{Extraction and Verification}\label{sec:extraction}

Lower bounds are certified only after converting a candidate point to a binary coloring and verifying directly that no forbidden monochromatic cliques remain. In our implementation this conversion is deterministic and is performed by the method of conditional expectation. The choice is not cosmetic, because it is what guarantees that the rounding step cannot make the relaxed objective worse.

Starting from a relaxed iterate $x \in [0,1]^m$, we process edges one at a time in an arbitrary order. For the current undecided edge $e$, we evaluate the objective conditioned on fixing $x_e=0$ and on fixing $x_e=1$, keeping the remaining undecided edges at their current fractional values, and permanently assign the value yielding the smaller conditional objective. Because $M$ is multilinear, and therefore affine in each coordinate, we have
\[
    M(x) \;=\; x_e\, M(x \mid x_e = 1) \;+\; (1-x_e)\, M(x \mid x_e = 0),
\]
so at least one of the two endpoints has value no larger than the current conditional expectation. Iterating this argument over all edges produces a binary coloring $\widehat{x} \in \{0,1\}^m$ with
\[
    M(\widehat{x}) \;\le\; M(x).
\]
Since $M(\widehat{x})$ is a nonnegative integer, this has a sharp consequence: any relaxed point with $M(x) < 1$ is rounded to a valid Ramsey witness with $M(\widehat{x}) = 0$. Driving the relaxed objective strictly below one is therefore sufficient to obtain a certificate, even though the relaxed problem itself is nonconvex and we have no global optimization guarantee. We finally verify $\widehat{x}$ directly by exhaustive clique checking, or equivalently by running a max-clique routine on the graph and its complement; absence of a forbidden monochromatic $K_r$ or $K_s$ certifies the claimed lower bound.

This conditional-expectation step is the bridge between the continuous relaxation and the discrete certificate: the optimizer produces fractional information about how monochromatic cliques are being avoided in expectation, and conditional expectation turns that information into an exact candidate witness without ever increasing the violation count.

\section{Results}

We evaluate the method on diagonal and near-diagonal Ramsey instances; the global picture is summarized in \cref{tab:small-ramsey-grid}. The pipeline matches the best known lower bound across a broad swath of the small-Ramsey table: it certifies $R(3,k) > L(3,k)$ for all $k \leq 8$ (where $L(r,s)$ denotes the current best known lower bound), recovers the exact values $R(4,4) = 18$ and $R(4,5) = 25$, and most importantly reproduces $R(5,5) \geq 43$ by repeatedly finding $42$-vertex colorings with no monochromatic $K_5$ in either color. The $R(5,5)$ result is the headline: it matches the strongest known construction at that size~\cite{exoo1989lower} and is a bound that recent LLM-driven evolutionary search did not recover~\cite{nagda2026reinforcedgenerationcombinatorialstructures}. The current pipeline does not yet produce a witness at the target size for $R(4,6)$, where our best run has $5$ monochromatic cliques remaining on $35$ vertices, and $R(4,7)$ and $R(5,6)$ are out of reach at present at our compute budget; we discuss these cases below.

\paragraph{Many witnesses.}
A striking feature of the $R(5,5)$ runs is that the method does not converge to a single isolated solution. Across independent restarts it produces a large collection of distinct $42$-vertex certificates, indicating that the optimization landscape contains a nontrivial family of high-quality constructions rather than a single basin. \cref{fig:r55-results} visualizes this as a histogram of how often each distinct certificate (identified up to isomorphism by its graph6 string) was encountered; the corresponding graphs are listed in \cref{tab:results}. The shape of the histogram is informative: a small number of colorings are recovered in abundance, while a long tail of colorings is found only one or a few times. So far the method has discovered $80$ of the $328$ known $42$-vertex colorings. We do not expect to recover all of them with the current uniform initialization; designing initialization priors that bias the search toward under-represented basins is a natural direction for future work.

The cases the method does not yet solve are equally informative. \paragraph{Positioning.}
These results support the paper's central claim: Frank-Wolfe style continuous optimization is a competitive heuristic for producing certified discrete witnesses on Ramsey instances, with current evidence strongest on diagonal and near-diagonal cases. The comparison to LLM-driven Ramsey search should be framed carefully. Recent AlphaEvolve-based work improves or matches many classical lower bounds and is especially strong on larger off-diagonal instances~\cite{nagda2026reinforcedgenerationcombinatorialstructures}. Our method is not an apples-to-apples replacement for that meta-search pipeline; rather, it provides a much simpler, optimization-centered route that already recovers nontrivial witnesses --- including $R(5,5) \geq 43$, which AlphaEvolve did not --- without imposing algebraic or circulant restrictions on the edge variables.

\subsection{Guarantees for the Raw Multilinear Objective}



The relaxed problem in \cref{eq:relaxed-problem} is nonconvex, so we should not expect global convergence guarantees from first-order optimization alone. Nevertheless, the raw multilinear objective has several useful structural properties that make the relaxation meaningful for certificate search.

\paragraph{No integrality gap.}
The relaxation introduces no integrality gap for the raw objective $M$. More generally, let $F:[0,1]^m\to\mathbb{R}$ be any multilinear function. Since $F$ is affine in each coordinate separately, fixing all other coordinates and minimizing over one coordinate always admits an endpoint minimizer. Repeating this argument coordinate by coordinate gives
\[
    \min_{x\in[0,1]^m} F(x)
    =
    \min_{x\in\{0,1\}^m} F(x).
\]
Thus the continuous relaxation of $M$ is exact in the global-optimum sense: any fractional global optimum can be converted to a binary global optimum without increasing the expected number of violations. This does not make the optimization problem easy, but it rules out the usual failure mode where the relaxation is intrinsically too weak.

\paragraph{Conditional-expectation extraction.}
The same coordinate-wise multilinearity gives a deterministic rounding guarantee. For any fractional point $x\in[0,1]^m$, process the edges in an arbitrary order. When edge $e$ is considered, compare the value of $M$ after fixing $x_e=0$ with the value after fixing $x_e=1$, keeping all unprocessed edges at their current fractional values, and choose the better endpoint. Because $M$ is affine in $x_e$,
\[
    M(x)
    =
    x_e M(x\mid x_e=1)
    +
    (1-x_e) M(x\mid x_e=0),
\]
so at least one endpoint has value no larger than the current conditional expectation. Iterating over all edges produces a binary coloring $\widehat{x}$ satisfying
\[
    M(\widehat{x}) \le M(x).
\]
Since $M(\widehat{x})$ is a nonnegative integer, any relaxed point with $M(x)<1$ rounds to a valid Ramsey witness with $M(\widehat{x})=0$.

\paragraph{Frank-Wolfe stationarity.}
For exact-gradient optimization of a smooth objective over a compact convex domain, nonconvex Frank-Wolfe has a standard convergence guarantee in terms of the Frank-Wolfe gap \cite{lacostejulien2016convergence,braun2022conditional}. In the hypercube formulation for the raw objective, this gap is
\[
    g_M(x)
    =
    \max_{s\in[0,1]^m}
    \langle x-s,\nabla M(x)\rangle .
\]
Under standard step-size rules, exact Frank-Wolfe guarantees
\[
    \min_{0\leq t<T} g_M(x_t) = O(T^{-1/2})
\]
for the smooth polynomial objective $M$. This is a convergence-to-stationarity statement, not a guarantee of finding a global Ramsey witness. Its role here is more modest: it provides a principled continuous optimization baseline, while the certificate guarantee above explains what is sufficient for successful rounding.

\paragraph{Relation to continuous greedy.}
The method is mechanically related to continuous-greedy algorithms for submodular maximization, which also optimize multilinear extensions through repeated linear optimization over a relaxation polytope followed by rounding \cite{calinescu2011maximizing}. The analogy should not be read as a transfer of approximation guarantees. Classical continuous greedy applies to monotone submodular maximization, while specialized non-monotone DR-submodular variants require assumptions and update rules that are not the ones used here \cite{bian2017continuous}. In this paper, the connection is only geometric: conditional-gradient oracles repeatedly return extreme combinatorial atoms, biasing the relaxed trajectory toward colorings that can be extracted and certified.

\subsection{Detailed batching scheme}

For batching, it is convenient to work with the clique-averaged objective
\[
\bar{L}(x)
:=
\frac{1}{|\mathcal{C}_r|}
\sum_{C \in \mathcal{C}_r} \ell_r(C;x)
+
\frac{1}{|\mathcal{C}_s|}
\sum_{C \in \mathcal{C}_s} \ell_s(C;x),
\]
whose gradient has the same form as the original objective up to a constant rescaling.

The diagonal case $r=s$ is straightforward. Both color terms are indexed by the same family of $r$-cliques, so we sample a single batch $S \subseteq \mathcal{C}_r$ and evaluate both colors on that same set:
\[
\widehat{L}_{\mathrm{diag}}(x)
:=
\frac{1}{|S|}
\sum_{C \in S} \bigl(\ell_r(C;x) + \ell_s(C;x)\bigr).
\]
This is an unbiased estimator of $\bar{L}(x)$. Using the same batch for both colors is also the most intuitive choice: each stochastic update compares the red and blue penalties on exactly the same local vertex pattern, rather than adding variance from two unrelated samples.

The off-diagonal case $r \neq s$ requires one more design choice because the two color terms live on different clique families. Here the main question is not whether to batch, but how to organize the two batches across an epoch.

Let
\[
R = \max(r,s), \qquad q = \min(r,s),
\]
and let $\ell_R$ and $\ell_q$ denote the clique penalties for the corresponding two colors. We choose a batch size $B_R$ for the larger clique family $\mathcal{C}_R$, and define
\[
T = \left\lceil \frac{|\mathcal{C}_R|}{B_R} \right\rceil
\]
to be the number of optimization steps in one epoch. At the start of the epoch, we randomly permute all $R$-cliques and partition them into batches
\[
A_1,\dots,A_T,
\qquad |A_t| \leq B_R.
\]
We then independently permute the smaller clique family $\mathcal{C}_q$ and partition it into the same number $T$ of batches
\[
B_1,\dots,B_T,
\qquad |B_t| \approx \frac{|\mathcal{C}_q|}{T}.
\]
Equivalently, the smaller-clique batch size is chosen so that both colors finish one full pass through their respective clique families at the same time.

At optimization step $t$, we evaluate one batch from each color and then apply a single update:
\[
\widehat{\mathcal{L}}_t(x)
:=
\frac{1}{|A_t|}
\sum_{C \in A_t} \ell_R(C;x)
\;+\;
\frac{1}{|B_t|}
\sum_{D \in B_t} \ell_q(D;x).
\]
This is what ``independent batching'' means in our epoch-based implementation. The two color classes are sampled independently, but they are synchronized at the level of epochs: each step uses the $t$-th batch from the large-clique pass and the $t$-th batch from the small-clique pass, and after $T$ steps both passes are complete.

This viewpoint is often clearer than thinking of each update as drawing two fresh random mini-batches from scratch. Over one epoch, every clique in each family is visited exactly once, up to the usual rounding in the last batch, and reshuffling happens only when the next epoch begins. The resulting estimator is still unbiased for the same clique-averaged objective, while fitting naturally with the ``one pass through all cliques per epoch'' interpretation used in the implementation.
